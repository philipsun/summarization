[1] Disputes between ethnic groups broke out in parts of the Soviet Union two years before its dissolution. [2] Gorbachev decried the unrest, but admitted that early Soviet efforts to fashion a unified culture out of a melange of more than 100 ethnic groups using 100 languages was a cause of the current troubles. [3] In the two years preceding June 1990 the Kremlin had dispatched troops to quell fighting in 13 regions. [4] There had been unrest in Kirghizia and Uzbekistan, and serious fighting in Georgia, Azerbaijan and Kazakhstan [5] The demise of the Soviet Union touched off new ethnic conflicts. [6] Those already underway worsened. [7] Armenia and Azerbaijan fought over an enclave of Armenians within Azerbaijan. [8] A group in South Ossetia wanted out of Georgia. [9] Minorities of ethnic Russians and Ukrainians fought for rights in Moldavia. [10] In late 1992 Russia sent specially trained troops to halt fighting in the Caucasus Mountains and in Tajikistan. [11] The dangerous conflicts in the former Soviet Union worried the international community. [12] There was fear that the area would be destabilized. [13] By May 1994 the number killed in Tajikistan reached 100,00. [14] The Communist Party leader said only a restored Soviet Union could deal with the ethnic fighting.
