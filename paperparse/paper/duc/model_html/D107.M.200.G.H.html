[1] In mid-July, 1988's drought looked record-breaking. [2] Predictions were for month-long hot, dry weather in grain-growing states and rain elsewhere, and a 4% chance of three- month recovery based on past-drought analysis. [3] Rain was insufficient. [4] Severe storms brought tornadoes, hail damage to crops, and lightning-sparked forest fires. [5] Temperatures topped 100 and states issued heat alerts. [6] Weather experts attending a Drought Symposium urged continued attention to climate, and energy policies to reduce the greenhouse effect. [7] One theorized a relationship between drought and Jupiter's orbit. [8] Crop declines were forecast and grain futures dropped. [9] Adequate domestic and export supplies were expected. [10] Higher prices would reduce consumption. [11] Poor, food-importing countries would suffer most. [12] Good harvests in Europe and Asia were projected. [13] Georgia itemized hay sources. [14] UPS, railroads, and truckers transported hay to Kentucky and Ohio for free. [15] Congress proposed up to $100,000 aid to farmers. [16] States provided funds for farm loans. [17] President Reagan visited drought areas. [18] Easing of 1989 crop acreage controls was considered. [19] Farmers sued a company reneging on drought insurance. [20] Drinking water was hauled to towns. [21] Dam flow reductions endangered neighbors' supplies. [22] Catch limits disappeared at reservoirs. [23] Gamefish and turkeys were depleted. [24] Livestock sales increased. [25] Cannery jobs fell. [26] Laying off mill workers and banning logging was considered. [27] Few rain interruptions accelerated highway projects.
