[1] On July 17, 1990 Saddam Hussein accused Kuwait of violating OPEC production quotas and of stealing oil from wells along the countries' disputed border. [2] Tension increased July 24-25 as the U.S. announced a "short-notice exercise" in the Persian Gulf and Iraq said it would not be intimidated by U.S. pressure. [3] Tension eased when Iraq and Kuwait agreed to talks, but by the time the parties met on July 31, Iraq had massed 100,000 troops on the Kuwaiti border. [4] The next day Iraq pulled out of the talks and on August 2,1990 invaded and quickly occupied all of Kuwait. [5] President Bush froze Iraqi assets in the U.S. and blocked most Iraqi imports. [6] The U.N. [7] Security Council denounced the invasion and demanded Iraq's withdrawal. [8] The Pentagon stepped up air patrols and movement of warships into the Persian Gulf. [9] When, on August 6, the U.S. pushed strong sanctions against Iraq through the U.N. [10] Security Council, Iraq struck back, rounding up several hundred Britons, Americans, and West Germans in Kuwait, taking some of them to Iraq. [11] Iraqi troop concentrations menaced the Saudi Arabian border and the U.S. warned Iraq of vital American interests in Saudi Arabia, but as of August 6, 1990 it was a stand-off. [12] Iraq was in possession of Kuwait and oil prices soared on the international market.
