[1] Physicist Andrei D. Sakharov became the youngest member of the Soviet Academy of Sciences and helped develop the Soviet hydrogen bomb, but urged limiting its production and testing. [2] He was a prominent 1970s dissident and won the 1975 Nobel Peace Prize. [3] In January 1980, after Sakharov criticized Soviet intervention in Afghanistan, Breshnev exiled him to Gorky. [4] Gorbachev allowed Sakharov and his wife Yelena Bonner to return to Moscow in December 1986. [5] There they lived simply in a two-room, seventh-floor apartment which became a mecca for Soviets seeking aid. [6] Sakharov supported Gorbachev's reforms but called for speedier progress in human rights, political prisoner liberation, and a nuclear testing ban. [7] In November 1988 he visited the US, underwent medical tests, and promoted a world peace foundation. [8] In April 1989, he was elected to parliament after Soviet physicists voted secretly to nominate him. [9] He warned of national economic catastrophe. [10] Sakharov died alone in his Moscow apartment in December 1989 at the age of 68, of an apparent heart attack, hours after urging creation of an opposition party. [11] World and Soviet leaders and press eulogized him as a sincere man who suffered great injustice and helped change Soviet society. [12] 45,000 grievers filed past Sakharov's body. [13] Sakharov had four children and four grandchildren.
