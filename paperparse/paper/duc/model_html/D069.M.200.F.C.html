[1] Through the first three months of 1989, 80,00 East Germans fled their country, and massive demonstrations raised doubts that Erich Honeker the, hard-line Communist leader could resist the democratic reforms sweeping his country. [2] Street demonstrations occurred throughout East Germany in November 1989, and the East German Communist Party was forced to admit the its system had failed. [3] The West German constitution called for a united Germany, but it received little attention until late 1989. [4] The specter of a powerful undivided Germany troubled many, but particularly the Soviets and Poles. [5] Matters moved quickly in early 1990. [6] Soviet President Gorbachev gave West Germany an unequivocal promise that German reunification would be honored. [7] Two days later, on the 12th of February, said that negotiations with East Germany should begin as soon as possible after the 18 March East German elections. [8] In time the World War II Allies would endorse reunification. [9] With reunification imminent, world Jewish leaders asked for guarantees that the Holocaust would not be repeated. [10] Most prominent American historians agreed that a stable Germany was a lesser threat to stability than the woeful conditions and near chaos in East Germany.
