[1] Overloading is a major contributor to ferry and passenger boat accidents, particularly in the third world. [2] Ferries in India, Guatemala, Egypt, China and Bangladesh all carried far more passengers than authorized when they sank. [3] Captain error also contributed to accidents. [4] In Guatemala, a ferry captain was charges with negligent homicide when his boat which was being towed, sank after he failed to steer it. [5] A ferry sank off the Egyptian coast after inexplicably leaving standard navigational channels and ramming a coral reef in stormy seas. [6] In that accident, survivors claimed that crewmembers abandoned ship without helping them. [7] The ship's engineer denied the charges. [8] Two boats carrying Somali refugees sank off the coast of Kenya, resulting in more than 140 deaths, although the cause of the sinking was not reported. [9] An overcrowded ferry in Bangladesh sank with almost double the passengers it was authorized. [10] In a separate ferry incident in Bangladesh, pirates robbed passengers and threw six overboard. [11] In the Indian accident, monsoon rains may have contributed as well as overcrowding. [12] Even an advanced country such as Israel can experience ferry accidents. [13] Nineteen U.S. sailors died when an Israeli ferry sank while returning the sailors to the USS Saratoga. [14] No cause was reported.
