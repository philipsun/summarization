[1] George Bush's choice of Indiana Senator Dan Quayle as his running mate, surprised most political observers. [2] Despite service in Congress and Senate, he was looked upon as lacking the experience and mettle for a vigorous national campaign. [3] Several Republican senators rallied support. [4] Senator John Warner of Virginia compared Quayle to Thomas Jefferson. [5] Senator Bob Dole in nominating Quayle at the Republican Convention, called him a dedicated public servant and devoted family man and cited his support for the Reagan program. [6] Devotion to family and friends was a theme voiced by those who had known Quayle for many years. [7] Relatives and friends believed that if Quayle were elected, he and his family would remain a strong unit. [8] Questions arose regarding Quayle's fitness for the job, and there were some suggestions that he should withdraw. [9] He had called himself a "Vietnam-era veteran" which angered some actual combat veterans. [10] Quayle's service was in the National Guard, a position he apparently gained through family influence. [11] Quayle's resume contained a reference to a position held in Indiana, two years before it was created. [12] Quayle survived these criticisms but did not play a strong role in the campaign or as vice president. [13] Friends, especially those in his hometown, remained loyal.
