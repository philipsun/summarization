[1] On Tuesday, April 24, 1990, shuttle Discovery carried the 12.5-ton Hubble Space Telescope 380 miles into space. [2] Once in orbit above Earth's distorting atmosphere, Hubble would be able to see 10 times better than the largest Earth telescopes. [3] Wednesday it was lifted into space by Discovery's mechanical arm and released into orbit. [4] Friday the 94.5-inch mirror was exposed to starlight. [5] Saturday controllers took Hubble out of safe mode. [6] Problems with a solar power array, communications antennas, and two position- stabilizing gyroscopes -- exacerbated by conservative limits set on systems -- put Hubble several days behind schedule and delayed transmittal of the first photograph. [7] Discovery's astronauts -- Shriver, Bolden, Hawley, McCandless, and Sullivan - - were all veterans of previous flights. [8] The shuttle followed Hubble for two days to assist if necessary. [9] A seven- month checkout period would follow. [10] Spacelab Astro was to be put up two weeks later to give Hubble additional target information. [11] Hubble, delayed since 1983, cost $1.5 billion. [12] Spare parts, training, and software cost $500 million more. [13] Maintenance cost $7 million a month on Earth and will cost $200 million yearly in orbit, for Hubble's 15-year lifetime. [14] Discovery carried the Hooker Telescope eyepiece used by astronomer Edwin Hubble, for whom the Hubble is named.
