[1] A book entitled, "Wal-Mart: Sam Walton's Retail Phenomenon" was published two years after Walton died. [2] It was advertised as a "scholarly account" of Wal-Mart's success. [3] The chain's rise was spectacular. [4] The company did not incorporate until 1971 when it had only 38 outlets and was not listed among the country's top 71 discounters. [5] Its turnover was nine percent of Sears' and 11 percent of K-Mart's. [6] Ten years later its sales surpassed both. [7] Walton studied the competition and adapted from them. [8] Another strength was its openness to technology. [9] An example was its 20 million dollar investment in an internal satellite system.
